Technology and Sustainability (image?)
Introduction:

Imagine for a moment that you have two bank accounts. In one account there is money. In the other account there is a Personal Carbon Allowance (PCA) representing the amount of Co2 you are allowed to release into the atmosphere each year. When you book a plane ticket to your favorite destination you pay for your ticket with money, but in addition you will have to pay in kg of carbon emitted into the atmosphere during your flight. Flying from Oslo to London will set you back, 1200NOK and 241kg of Co2 (an Iphone will cost you approx. 70 kg of Co2).

This scheme places the responsibility of pollution in the hands of the consumer and, in addition to existing emission quotas on a national level, could help reach the goal of achieving the UN sustainable development goals.

If you live an environmentally friendly lifestyle, or simply do not have the money to do otherwise, you can sell your unspent allowance on the open market (trade Kg of Co2 for money). The PCA scheme will limit your personal freedom to travel, shop and consume. Your allowance, distributed by the government once a year, will decrease slowly over time in accordance with the national quota, but PCA could lead to a redistribution of wealth and make emissions more visible to the consumer.

Sustainability: Meeting the needs of the current population without compromising the need of future generations. UN sustainabile development goals: Objectives that are part of a blueprint for peace and prosperity for all humans on earth. Kate Raworth’s donut: Shows how humanity can develop and thrive in sustainable way by staying between the outer planetary boundary and the inner social foundation. Planned obsolescence: Designing a product to become undesirable or unfunctional after a planned amount of time.

step 2

2. Choose the three UN Sustainability goals that is most relevant (positive/negative) to the PCA scheme: Are there conflicts between such a scheme and the SDGs?

• Nr 1: No poverty • Nr 12: Reasonable consumption and production • Nr 13: Climate action A harmful side effect of this scheme is that it could hurt goal 1, which is to eliminate poverty. The reason why is that it could hurt economic development and increase the threshold one need to overcome to escape poverty. This scheme has a positive effect on goal 12, since it both gives companies an incentive to use less CO2 in their products and services and it reduces the consumption of costumers. The scheme is directly aimed to limit CO2 emitted, which make it a big step for taking climate action, goal 13.

step 4

6. Make up your mind! Will this make society and the environment better or worse:

It is very difficult to determine the scheme is going to have an overall positive or negative impact on the environment and society. The PCA does force individuals to limit CO2 and consume less which is better for the environment and the climate. It encourages efficiency and more clean energy, because the amount of CO2 one is allowed to spend is directly dependent on the society's CO2-quotas. If a product has a smaller carbon footprint, it is then less expensive in terms of PCA. It could also have some serious unintentional consequences. Since people must consume less, this will hurt the global demand for products. If the demand decreases and there is enough of supply, this could hurt the innovation and desire for creating new products and technology. The scheme is also a form of oppression, since it allows the government to have access to the population’s economic activities, which is a trait more associated with totalitarian regimes. The PCA would most likely have positive consequences for the environment by limiting human consumption and putting a financial incentive to make CO2-frendly products. The negative consequences will be presented socially and economically.

step 5

7. Identify potential consequences: What about individual freedom? What about political freedom? Could it be misused? Do privacy concerns outweigh positive effects on climate? Are there social consequences? Are there economic consequences?

Individual freedom: The PCA scheme needs to be enforced somehow and the government do need access to the population’s banking information. This compromises the freedom of individuals and can make them feel oppressed. Political freedom: Someone need to assign the value of PCA and there is a risk this is done with political motives behind them. Politicians can allocate bad carbon behavior with the goals and policies of their opposition and declare them illegal. Misuse: Corrupt politicians can accept bribes from certain companies and make their products and services more PCA-cheap and do the opposed to their competitors. Privacy vs climate: Climate change is a serious issue that can have devastating consequences. It might be necessary to limit privacy in order to combat climate change. Humans are short-sighted and can be greedy, and it is possible it could doom civilization if freedom is not limited. On the other hand, it is not obvious if the PCA will work or if it is the best solution. Climate change is also not the only challenge humanity need to worry about, a pandemic, a super volcano or an asteroid could potentially end humanity. Limiting privacy and freedom, makes it harder to grow and obtain the technology and know how to deal with future problems.
